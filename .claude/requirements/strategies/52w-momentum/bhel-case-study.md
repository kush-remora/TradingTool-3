# BHEL Case Study — Observing Accumulation in Raw Data

## Why BHEL?

BHEL (Bharat Heavy Electricals) is a large Indian infrastructure company listed on NSE. We chose it as our source case because it produced a clear accumulation period followed by a strong breakout in 2026, and we have clean daily data to study it.

The stock returned +52% over 12 months, but almost the entire gain happened in a short burst after a long quiet period. That quiet period is what we are documenting here.

**Important:** BHEL is the source case, not the definition. Every observation below is meant to help us identify signals that generalise to other stocks — not create rules that only fit BHEL.

---

## How this document is structured

We do not present all observations at once. Instead, we start with the simplest thing we noticed and measure it. Each observation leaves an open question — and the next observation answers it. By the end, we have a set of signals that together identify accumulation with confidence that no single signal could provide alone.

---

## The story in brief

Between January and March 2026, BHEL went through two major sell-offs — large volumes of shares sold in single days, driving the price down sharply. After the second one, the stock went quiet for 37 days. Then in April it launched.

| Period | Price Range | Avg Daily Volume | What happened |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jan 8–9 | ₹303 → ₹267 | 55M – 63M | First major sell-off |
| Jan 12–30 | ₹242 → ₹265 | ~12M | Price settling |
| Feb 1–10 | ₹252 → ₹276 | ~8M | Brief quiet |
| Feb 11–12 | ₹276 → ₹255 | 85M – 69M | Second major sell-off |
| Feb 13 – Mar 30 | ₹247 → ₹267 | ~9M | The quiet period |
| Apr – May 2026 | ₹247 → ₹424 | Surging | +71% in 10 weeks |

---

## Observation 1 — Price range compressed

**What we noticed:** Looking at the chart from Jan to March, the stock appeared to stop moving. We measured it.

**The data:** From Feb 13 to Mar 30 (37 trading days), the stock moved between ₹247 and ₹267 — a range of ₹20, or about 8%. For an actively traded stock, this is very tight. Many stocks cover that distance in a single week.

**The open question this raises:**

Looking at the table above, a careful reader will notice that the price range from Jan 12–30 also looks similarly tight (₹242–₹265). So why do we call Feb 13 onwards the accumulation window and not Jan 12?

Price range alone cannot answer this. It is the first signal we noticed — but it is incomplete on its own. The next observation starts to resolve this.

**What price compression tells us — and what it doesn't:**

When a stock's price stops moving after a major sell-off, it means sellers are no longer in control. If they were, price would keep falling. The compression is a sign that every share being sold is being quietly absorbed.

But compression can also happen for other reasons — low market interest, a pause before another sell-off, or just temporary calm. Price range alone cannot distinguish between these. We need more signals.

---

## Observation 2 — Price drifted slowly downward the entire time

**What we noticed:** The overall range was tight, but the price was not flat. It was slowly and steadily declining across the entire Jan 12 – Mar 30 period.

**The data:** Looking at closing prices at key points:

| Date | Closing Price |
|---|---|
| Jan 12 | ₹267.90 |
| Jan 30 | ₹262.70 |
| Feb 1 | ₹252.10 |
| Feb 13 | ₹255.75 |
| Mar 30 | ₹245.50 |

From ₹267.90 to ₹245.50 — a slow, steady decline of ₹22 (about 8%) over 11 weeks. No sharp drops. No recovery back to the starting level. Just a quiet grind lower, week after week.

**What this reveals:**

Think about what a slow drift down actually means. If sellers were truly in control, price would drop sharply — they would be unloading urgently and price would crater. That did not happen here. If buyers were in control, price would hold or rise. That did not happen either.

What we see instead is sellers exiting slowly and quietly — not in panic, just gradually letting go — while buyers absorb every share without pushing price back up. Buyers are not chasing. They are patient. They let the price drift slightly lower each week, giving sellers the exit they need, while quietly building their own position.

This slow drift is not a sign of weakness. It is a deliberate process. Large buyers do not want price to rise while they are still accumulating — that would make their remaining purchases more expensive. So they absorb, wait, let it drift a little, absorb again.

**Why this is important:**

This observation tells us something Observation 1 could not. A flat price range of 8% could mean many things — including that nobody cares about the stock at all. But a slow, orderly drift down within that range, without any panic or sharp acceleration, is a more specific signal. It tells us the selling is controlled and the buying is deliberate.

**The open question this raises:**

We can see the price was calm and drifting slowly. But both observations so far are about price alone. We still cannot confirm whether meaningful buying was actually happening — or whether the stock was simply being ignored. The next observation looks at whether individual days also behaved calmly, before we get to volume.

---

## Observation 3 — No single day behaved erratically

**What we noticed:** Not only was the overall price range tight, each individual day was calm too. No single day jumped 5% up or dropped 6% down.

**The data:** We measured two things for every trading day across three periods:

- **H-L %** — how wide the price range was within a single day (high minus low, as a percentage)
- **O-C %** — how much the price moved from open to close (the directional move of the day)

| Metric | Jan 12–30 | Feb 1–13 | Feb 13–Mar 30 |
|---|---|---|---|
| Avg H-L % | 3.77% | 3.03% | 3.37% |
| Avg O-C % (up days) | +1.64% | +1.22% | +1.43% |
| Avg O-C % (down days) | -2.06% | -1.47% | -1.67% |
| Up / Down days | 5 / 8 | 6 / 5 | 22 / 19 |

**Being honest about what this shows:**

The numbers across all three periods are not dramatically different. Feb 1–13 actually has the lowest average H-L% of the three. If someone argued that daily move behavior looks similarly calm from Jan 12 all the way to Mar 30, the data would support them.

This observation does not cleanly separate Feb 13 onwards from the earlier period. What it does tell us is that the entire Jan 12 – Mar 30 window had no erratic days — no panic selling, no urgent buyers. That is useful: it rules out periods where the market is clearly unsettled. But it cannot pinpoint where accumulation specifically begins.

**What this tells us — and what it doesn't:**

The absence of erratic days means no one is in a rush to sell or buy. That is a necessary condition for accumulation — you cannot accumulate quietly in a volatile stock. But calm alone is not proof of accumulation. A stock can be calm simply because nobody cares about it.

**The honest conclusion after two observations:**

Price range and daily move behavior together suggest the entire Jan 12 – Mar 30 period is worth investigating. We cannot yet say where accumulation specifically starts, or whether it is happening at all versus simple neglect. We need to look at volume next.

---

## Observation 4 — Volume was slowly dying across the entire period

**What we noticed:** Across the entire Jan 12 – Mar 30 period, the number of shares traded each day kept making new lows. Not every day — but the quietest days kept getting quieter over time.

**The data:** We looked at day-over-day volume direction and found recurring streaks of consecutive lower-volume days. Within each streak, volumes descended. And the floor of each streak kept dropping over time:

| Streak | Volumes within streak | Floor |
|---|---|---|
| Jan 12 (4 days) | 36.4M → 10.9M → 9.5M → 5.6M | 5.6M |
| Jan 20 (3 days) | 24.9M → 14.5M → 6.2M | 6.2M |
| Jan 28 (3 days) | 8.3M → 7.8M → 6.3M | 6.3M |
| Feb 24 (3 days) | 10.7M → 8.9M → 6.5M | 6.5M |
| Mar 13 (4 days) | 11.2M → 8.0M → 7.1M → 4.2M | **4.2M ← new absolute low** |
| Mar 23 (3 days) | 11.7M → 7.4M → 6.8M | 6.8M |

Overall: **34 out of 59 days (58%) had lower volume than the previous day** — consistent across both Jan and Feb–Mar periods.

**Being honest about what this shows:**

Both periods (Jan 1–Feb 10 and Feb 11–Mar 30) show almost identical patterns — ~58% of days lower than the previous day, longest streak of 4 days each. The data does not cleanly separate one period from the other. The entire Jan 12 – Mar 30 window behaves the same way.

**What this reveals:**

Think of it simply. When fewer and fewer people are willing to trade a stock each day, it means the urgency to sell is fading. Nobody is rushing for the exit. The stock is becoming quieter with each passing week — not because it is being ignored, but because the sellers who were present earlier have mostly finished selling.

The floor was not a clean straight line down — the middle streaks (6.2M, 6.3M, 6.5M) were actually slightly higher than the first (5.6M). That is normal. The pattern is not about every single streak being lower than the last. It is about the overall direction across 11 weeks: the very first quiet spell reached 5.6M, and the very last reached 4.2M. The trend is down, even if it is noisy in the middle. This is a progressive process, not a mechanical one.

**Why this is important:**

This is the first observation that gives a directional answer to the question the previous ones left open: is the calm due to neglect or exhaustion of sellers? A stock that nobody cares about has random, flat volume. A stock where sellers are progressively exhausting themselves shows this staircase pattern — each quiet spell goes a little quieter than the last.

The absolute low of 4.2M on Mar 18 is the clearest data point: that was the quietest single day in the entire 11-week period, happening near the end of it. Sellers were essentially gone.

**The open question this raises:**

We can see the price was calm, drifting slowly lower, individual days were orderly, and volume was progressively drying up. But we still have not answered one thing: was the price at a sensible level during all this, or was it in freefall that just looked orderly? The next observation checks where price was sitting relative to its long-term average.

---

## Observation 5 — Price gravitated toward its long-term average and stayed there

**What is the 200-day average?** Take the closing price for the last 200 trading days and compute the average. This is the long-term reference level — where the stock has spent most of its recent history. A price far above it means the stock is running hot. A price far below it means the stock is in distress. A price orbiting it means the stock is at equilibrium.

**What we noticed:** Looking at the full Jan 1 – Mar 30 period, the relationship between price and the 200-day average changed dramatically over time. It was not a consistent story — it had three distinct phases.

**The data (full 60 days):**

| Period | Price vs 200D avg | Close within ±2%? |
|---|---|---|
| Jan 1–19 (13 days) | +4% to +21% above | 0 of 13 |
| Jan 20 – Feb 12 (17 days) | -1% to +9%, volatile | 5 of 17 |
| Feb 13 – Mar 27 (29 days) | Tight orbit around it | **23 of 29 (79%)** |
| Mar 30 (1 day) | -4.5% below | 0 of 1 |

Overall: 28 of 60 days (47%) within ±2%. But that average masks the real story.

**What the three phases actually show:**

**Phase 1 — Jan 1–19:** Price was 5–21% above the 200-day average. The stock was still running on momentum from its Nov–Dec rally. The Jan 8 crash (₹303 → ₹271) started pulling it back down toward the average.

**Phase 2 — Jan 20 – Feb 12:** Price found the 200-day average for the first time (Jan 20: -0.75%, Jan 21: +0.07%). But it was unstable — bouncing 2–9% above before the Feb 11–12 shock pulled it back again. The market was still deciding where this stock belongs.

**Phase 3 — Feb 13 – Mar 27:** Price settled into a tight orbit around the 200-day average. 23 of 29 days within ±2%. The average itself was slowly rising (₹254 → ₹257). Price was no longer running above it or falling below it — it was sitting right on it, day after day.

This is the cleanest signal in all five observations. The other four (price range, daily calm, drift, volume) looked broadly similar across Jan and Feb–Mar. This one does not — it clearly identifies Feb 13 onwards as the distinct window.

**What this reveals:**

When a stock settles right on its 200-day average for weeks, it means neither side has conviction. Sellers cannot push it lower — the average acts as a floor. Buyers are not yet pushing it higher — they are still accumulating. The stock is in balance, waiting.

The 200-day average at ~₹254–257 was acting as a centre of gravity. Every time price drifted above ₹265, it came back. Every time it dipped toward ₹247, buyers stepped in. This is not random — it is the footprint of deliberate, patient buying at a known reference level.

**Why this is important:**

This observation does two things the others could not. First, it confirms the stock was not in freefall — it had a stable, rising reference level it kept returning to. Second, it gives us the clearest boundary for the accumulation window: Feb 13 onwards, not Jan 12. The 200-day average proximity is the signal that separates the two periods where everything else looked similar.

---

## What all five observations together show

At this point we have seen:

1. **Price range was tight** — overall movement was small across Jan 12 – Mar 30
2. **Price drifted slowly lower** — orderly, not panicked; sellers exiting gradually
3. **Each individual day was calm** — no erratic moves in either direction across the full period
4. **Volume was progressively drying up** — each quiet spell went quieter than the last, floor reached 4.2M by Mar 18
5. **Price orbited the 200-day average** — specifically from Feb 13 onwards, 23 of 29 days within ±2%

No single observation proves accumulation. Observations 1–4 describe a broadly quiet period from Jan 12 onwards. Observation 5 is the one that sharpens it — pointing specifically to Feb 13 – Mar 27 as the tightest accumulation window.

Together they describe a market where sellers were gradually finishing, buyers were quietly absorbing at a known price level, and the stock was holding equilibrium. That is what accumulation looks like in raw data.

What followed confirmed it: ₹247 → ₹424 in 10 weeks.
